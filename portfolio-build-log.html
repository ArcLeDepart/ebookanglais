00 — Foreword

The interesting part of a project is never the part you can see.

Across my final year of the BUT Informatique at IUT Lyon 1 (Bourg-en-Bresse campus), I built four systems on purpose unlike each other: one lives on a phone and turns chores into a quest log, one is an arcade game where the screen tries to overwhelm you, one is a website that interviews you out loud for a job, and one is a small robot that surveys a room and reconstructs it live.

What ties them together isn't a language or a framework — it's the work underneath. Deciding what to build, choosing tools that fit, and getting a real thing to run end to end, including the moments when the network drops, the microphone is missing, or a sensor lies to you.

CH·01

Ascend Life Quest

Turn the day into a quest log — and level yourself up for real.

Stack
Flutter Dart Kotlin Firebase Room · SQLite AI quest gen
TypeSolo + pair
PlatformAndroid
ModeOnline + offline-first
The goal

Habit apps feel like homework. Ascend Life Quest turns the same loop into a game: your day becomes a set of quests, finishing them earns XP, and that XP levels you up and moves you up a ranking. The pitch is simple — make progress visible, and make it feel earned.

What it does
  • AI-generated daily quests — a fresh, personal set each day rather than a fixed checklist.
  • XP, levels & rankings — climb a global leaderboard or a private one with friends.
  • A full social layer — friend requests, a friends list, and direct messaging between players.
  • Offline mode — a local quest bank keeps the loop alive for at least two days with no signal; only the AI generation pauses until you reconnect.
Under the hood

The app exists in two builds — a Flutter (Dart) version I built solo and a native Kotlin version built as a pair — which let me feel the real trade-offs between cross-platform and native rather than read about them. Firebase runs the accounts, the friends graph, the messaging and the live leaderboard. A Room database stores the offline quest bank on the device, so the core experience survives a dead connection, and quest generation calls an AI service whenever the phone is back online.

What I took from it

Building one product two ways forced me to separate the idea from the framework. The harder lesson was data: an offline-first app has to be designed that way from the first screen — you can't bolt resilience on at the end.

Game design · Godot
CH·02

Godot Survivor

Stay alive — the screen won't make it easy.

Stack
Godot GDScript 2D Wave spawner Power-up draft
TypePair · 2
GenreSurvivor / roguelite
LoopKill → loot → level
The goal

A survivor-style arcade game in the vein of the genre: drop the player into an arena and let the pressure build. The objective is the oldest one there is — last as long as you can while the enemy count keeps climbing.

What it does
  • Endless swarms — enemies arrive in growing numbers and bear down on you.
  • Loot & leveling — defeated enemies drop pickups; collect enough to level up mid-run.
  • Power-up choices — each level lets you draft an upgrade that changes how you fight.
Under the hood

Built in Godot, the game wires together the loops that make the genre tick: enemy spawning, real-time combat and collision, the loot-and-pickup system, the level-up power-up draft, and a difficulty curve that scales with elapsed time.

What I took from it

Game feel is its own engineering discipline. Spawn rates, pacing and the power-up economy are the difference between a run that feels satisfying and one that feels unfair — and they only reveal themselves by playing, tuning, and playing again.

Applied AI · Voice · Web — Team SAE
CH·03

Interview Simulator

A recruiter that never tires of you practising.

Stack
Python Gradio faster-whisper Edge-TTS Ollama · Llama 3 / Mistral SSH tunnel · Paramiko
TypeTeam SAE · 3
InterfaceWeb (local)
InputVoice or text
The goal

Let anyone rehearse a real job interview, out loud, before the one that counts. You tell it the role you're after and how prepared you want to be; it plays the recruiter.

What it does
  • Set the scene — enter the target role, choose 1–30 questions, and optionally drop in your CV (PDF or DOCX).
  • A recruiter that adapts — questions are tailored to the role and, if provided, to your actual background.
  • Speaks and listens — it reads each question aloud in a natural French voice; you answer by microphone or keyboard, switchable on every question.
  • A real verdict — at the end it returns a structured review: a score out of 10 plus written feedback on clarity, examples, structure, fit and more.
Under the hood

A Python app behind a Gradio interface orchestrates a small pipeline of services. Speech in runs through faster-whisper (speech-to-text); speech out uses Microsoft Edge-TTS — the fr-FR “Henri” voice — with a local pyttsx3 fallback when there's no internet. The questions and the final evaluation come from an LLM served by Ollama (Llama 3 / Mistral) running on a remote GPU machine, reached over an SSH tunnel (Paramiko) so a laptop never has to host the model. Uploaded CVs are parsed and summarised locally, and aren't kept after the session.

What I took from it

This was the most “real-world” of the four — four separate services (speech-to-text, text-to-speech, the language model, and file parsing) that all had to hand off cleanly, with graceful fallbacks for the moments a network or a microphone simply isn't there.

Team SAE — built with Léo Periou and Mathéo Drecq.

Embedded · Robotics · BLE
CH·04

Mapping Robot

Send a robot around the room; watch the map appear on your phone.

Stack
ESP32 Arduino · C++ BLE Sensors Companion app Live mapping
TypePair · 2
Hardware4-wheel rover
ControlAuto + manual
The goal

Have a small robot survey a classroom and rebuild its shape live on a phone — software, hardware and a radio link, all working together in one moving object.

What it does
  • Drives the perimeter — a four-wheeled ESP32 rover follows the walls using its sensors.
  • Streams over BLE — it sends what it sees to a paired phone over Bluetooth Low Energy.
  • Draws the map — a dedicated mobile app renders the floor plan in real time as the robot moves.
  • Saves & revisits — each room is stored and browsable in the app, and you can switch to manual driving to steer the survey yourself.
Under the hood

ESP32 firmware (Arduino / C++) reads the sensors, handles the drive, and acts as a BLE peripheral. The companion mobile app is the BLE central: it receives the stream, reconstructs and draws the floor plan, stores each surveyed room, and exposes the manual-control mode.

What I took from it

Hardware doesn't forgive. Timing, noisy sensor readings and a constrained radio link meant the real work was turning an unreliable physical signal into a clean, trustworthy map on a screen.
